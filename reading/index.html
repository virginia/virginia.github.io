Been in Clevetown for the week, working hard. Smashed through Michael Cunningham's latest, The Snow Queen on the trips to and fro – some beautiful passages of writing, but the characters lack real depth. This really resonated though:
Ahead of them looms the vast limestone bulk of the Metropolitan Museum, its stern, familiar brightness. Barrett thinks, as he always does when he approaches the museum, of what lies within: a more-than-adequate sampling of every instance in which human beings were inspired to do more than human beings can technically do, whether it's the summoning of life from the stubborn inanimacy of paint and canvas, or the hammering of gold into reliquary saints with ecstatic and tortured faces the size of dimes.
Which is why I follow @museumbot.

31 August 2014
From this New Yorker article about Vandana Shiva, and the controversy surrounding her absolutist attitude to genetically modified crops:
Sustaining [ten billion] people will require farmers to grow more food in the next seventy-five years than has been produced in all of human history.
I am guilty of having received and passed on the 'suicide seeds' story myself, and I'm embarrassed to have done so without further investigation. As always, the story is much more nuanced.
Actually, it seems like the practicalities and politics of food production are having a bit of a Moment this week: a quick look at MIT's aeroponics lab, high-tech plant protein and in vitro meat in Rethinking Eating, and Alice Waters interviewed on Al Jazeera (she comes off sounding a bit naive, to be honest, although I love her restaurant and recipes).
And I'm deep into What We See When We Read now, which is fun and weird and thought-provoking. It has a zine-ish tactile quality that I love.
ISIS understands, and is using, various forms of media in sophisticated ways.
Geordie Wood has a very good portfolio.
